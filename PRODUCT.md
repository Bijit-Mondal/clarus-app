# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Clarus serves the people responsible for a company's security and compliance posture, working across three overlapping roles on the same surface:

- **Compliance & security owners** who drive audit readiness — mapping controls, closing findings, and steering the program toward a passed audit.
- **Contributors** across the org (engineering, IT, HR, ops) who are assigned controls and asked to supply evidence, answer questions, or remediate gaps.
- **External auditors and assessors** who review evidence, raise findings, and sign off.

They arrive mid-task during focused work sessions: checking what needs attention before a review, uploading or requesting evidence, resolving an open finding, or preparing an auditor-facing response. Each role needs a different slice of the same data, so the interface must make "where am I, what's mine, and what's next" obvious without training.

## Product Purpose

Clarus is a compliance / GRC (governance, risk, and compliance) platform: it helps teams reach and stay audit-ready across frameworks like SOC 2, ISO 27001, and similar, by centralizing controls, evidence, findings, and auditor collaboration.

The near-term goal is a polished, scalable **app shell** — navigation, layout, design system, and credible placeholder views — that establishes a confident foundation before feature depth lands. Success looks like a coherent product frame where every screen feels intentional, readable, and trustworthy to a security buyer, and where real GRC workflows (controls, evidence, findings, reports) can drop in without reworking the chrome.

## Brand Personality

Confident · Modern · Effortless

Voice is assured and plain-spoken: say what matters, skip filler, and let structure carry authority. Modern and effortless mean the product feels light and current rather than heavy enterprise GRC — approachable without sacrificing credibility. The name *Clarus* (clarity) is the through-line: in a domain full of ambiguity and audit anxiety, users should always know where they are, what they can do, and what happened.

The visual language draws on **Windows 11 / Fluent Design** — layered surfaces, subtle depth, calm motion, and clean variable-weight typography (Segoe UI Variable) — adapted into a focused compliance product rather than an OS.

## Anti-references

- **Enterprise GRC clutter.** Dense gray tables with tiny text, cramped forms, endless nested settings, and audit-anxiety-inducing walls of data. Clarus should feel like a modern product, not legacy compliance software.
- **Generic SaaS scaffolding.** Cream backgrounds, gradient heroes, hero-metric templates, eyebrow kickers on every section, identical icon-card grids.
- **Over-designed chrome.** Decorative glassmorphism, paired border-plus-wide-shadow "ghost cards," oversized radii, motion for its own sake. (Fluent depth is earned and restrained, never decorative glass.)
- **Overly playful UI.** Sketchy illustrations, bouncy elastic motion, loud decorative color — undermines the trust a compliance tool must project.
- **Trust theater.** Fake precision, vanity dashboards, or security-buzzword decoration that isn't backed by real information.

## Design Principles

1. **Clarity is the product.** Hierarchy, labels, and layout answer "where am I?" and "what's next?" before the user has to think. In a high-stakes, ambiguous domain, reducing uncertainty is the core value.
2. **Earn trust through restraint.** Confidence comes from strong structure, precise typography, and calm surfaces — not decoration. A compliance tool must look as credible as the claims it helps prove.
3. **One surface, many roles.** Owners, contributors, and auditors share screens but need different slices. Design for role-appropriate focus and clear ownership, not one-size-fits-all density.
4. **Design serves the workflow.** Screens exist to move work forward — controls, evidence, findings, reviews. Navigation, states, and density follow the task, not a template.
5. **Foundation before features.** The shell and design system must scale; placeholder views should feel shippable and demo-credible, never throwaway.
6. **Reject category reflex.** Avoid default SaaS and legacy-GRC tropes alike; if it could belong to any compliance dashboard, rework it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**: sufficient color contrast (including placeholders and muted text), visible focus states, keyboard-operable controls, and semantic structure. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion` for any animation. Use color plus non-color cues for status and data visualization — critical in a compliance context where pass/fail, open/closed, and risk states must never rely on color alone.
